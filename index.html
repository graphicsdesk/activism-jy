What makes Columbia the ‘activist Ivy’?

A year after Columbia’s “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” swept the nation, student activists and professors reflect on the University’s history of activism.

By Isha Banerjee and Emily Pickering

May 6, 2025

Stella Ragas / Photo Editor

After its historic 1968 campus protests, Columbia earned a critical title: the “activist Ivy.” As Columbia became the epicenter of pro-Palestinian protests last spring, garnering attention from news organizations across the country, the University emerged again as a symbol of student activism.

In April 2024, pro-Palestinian protesters pitched tents on South Lawn, posted signs marking a “Liberated zone,” and occupied Hamilton Hall—a clear callback to the monumental 1968 protests. On April 30, 2024, 56 years after the New York Police Department arrested over 700 protesters and injured 148 on Columbia’s campus, the NYPD swept the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” and Hamilton Hall, marking the largest mass arrests on campus since 1968.

In 1968, protesters belonging to the Society of Afro-American Students and Students for a Democratic Society occupied five buildings, including the president’s office and Hamilton Hall. These occupations were in protest of the University’s plans to build a gym in Morningside Park, financial involvement in the Vietnam War, and participation in the Institute for Defense Analyses, a forum for universities and governments that fund military research to collaborate.

Though Columbia students continue to protest, with recent degree revocations, suspensions, and expulsions, student protesters have described a “chilling effect” on their activism. The federal government targeted Columbia, cancelling $400 million in federal funding and detaining Palestinian activists for their participation in pro-Palestinian protests.

Matthew Connelly, CC ’90, professor of international and global history, said there was “no denying” that the University was the “activist Ivy.”

“There are certain kinds of students; they want to be politically active, they want to be engaged, they’re not necessarily going to have the same kind of politics, but they want to be in a place where there are people who also have real commitments and care about national politics and world politics,” Connelly said. “I’m starting to think we should call ourselves the exciting Ivy.”

Columbia University in the City of New York

Columbia prides itself on its location—boasting its ties to New York City in the very name of the institution.

“The University recognizes the importance of its location in New York City and seeks to link its research and teaching to the vast resources of a great metropolis,” the Columbia website reads.

For some, one of the sources of Columbia’s activism is its expansion into West Harlem.

Columbia has continued to expand into Morningside Heights and West Harlem with the building of its Manhattanville campus. Several West Harlem residents and community leaders have voiced concerns about the University’s encroachment into Harlem, arguing that the University has gentrified the neighborhood.

“Columbia’s relationship to Harlem has been marked by a long history of displacement and gentrification, a process that mirrors its complicity and investment in Israel’s settler-colonial project,” Palestinian activist Maryam Alwan, GS ’25, wrote in a statement to Spectator. “The University’s expansion—especially through the Manhattanville campus project—has led to the displacement of long-standing Black and Brown communities under the guise of ‘urban renewal.’”

Photo by Ryan Murphy / Staff Photographer

In October 2023, Columbia restricted campus access to Columbia ID holders, preventing non-affiliates from accessing campus. These restrictions remain in place today. Throughout the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment,” the University maintained that the gates remained closed due to security and concerns about “outside agitators”—non-affiliates who joined the protests on campus.

“The fact that somebody has to be named an outside agitator suggests that there’s a world inside and there’s a world outside, and that’s the basic kind of structure of the institution, from the admissions office to the gates—there’s an inside and there’s an outside,” Reinhold Martin, professor of architecture and president of the Columbia chapter of the American Association of University Professors, said.

During the “Gaza Solidarity Encampment,” several protests featured chants and signs that advocated against Columbia’s expansion in Harlem.

Protesters from across the city arrived outside Columbia’s gates to show their solidarity with the encampment or voice concerns about the safety of Jewish students at Columbia. The NYPD arrested several protesters at these demonstrations.

Photo by Stella Ragas / Photo Editor

“The fact that people who want to protest, to join a protest effectively, or stand in solidarity with a protest outside the gates, if something’s happening inside the gates, I think it’s just the other side of that same coin,” Martin said. “It simply recognizes that this campus is in the city, and it exists in the world.”

The success of schools like the Journalism School is possible because the University is situated in New York City, Martin argued, stating that there is a “long history” of the newspaper business and media being concentrated in the city.

The setting of New York City also affects the type of students who choose to attend the University.

“New York City is a demanding place,” Mark Carnes, professor of history at Barnard, wrote in a statement to Spectator. “It attracts venturesome students who, by definition, are more likely to embrace controversy and contention.”

Columbia and the classroom

The “heart of the Columbia College education” is the Core Curriculum—a required set of classes all Columbia College students must take to graduate from the University.

A unique fixture of the Columbia experience, the Core began in 1919 as one course—Contemporary Civilization—which sought to prepare “students to grapple with ‘the insistent problems of the present.’”

During the protests, critics of the Core argued that the texts that students read in Contemporary Civilization had played a role in their “narrow” understanding of the world.

The Core also unifies the largest undergraduate college within the University. Contemporary Civilization readings discuss resistance, protest movements, and civil disobedience at length, requiring students to engage with activists like Mahatma Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., Frantz Fanon, and Barbara Fields.

“I didn’t intend to become an activist when I came to Columbia; I wanted to use my education to help people in my future endeavors,” Alwan wrote. “But I quickly realized that the concepts of human rights and anti-colonialism that I was learning about in my classes would be meaningless without immediate action to back them up.”

The Core also engages with current global issues. All Columbia College students must take Global Core courses, a requirement for which students take two classes that “engage directly with the variety of civilizations and the diversity of traditions that, along with the West, have formed the world and continue to interact in it today.”

Connelly emphasized the value of the Core in empowering students to teach themselves.

“If they really engage with this work in a critical way, including a self-critical way, I think they will come back with an even more profound self-awareness of where they are and where they might be,” Connelly said.

Hamilton houses the Center for the Core Curriculum, and many Columbia College students take a class in Hamilton within their first years at the University.

Hamilton Hall became infamous during the 1968 protests as one of five buildings student protesters occupied. Protesters also occupied or blockaded Hamilton Hall in 1972, 1985, and 1996, protesting war in Vietnam, speaking against South African apartheid, and advocating for the creation of an ethnic studies department. In 2024, protesters occupied the building again, declaring the building “liberated” and renaming it “Hind’s Hall” in honor of a Palestinian child killed by the Israeli military.

Photo by Stella Ragas / Photo Editor

“Hamilton Hall has become a symbolic site,” Martin said. “It probably has something to do, I think, if we look at this retrospectively, with its specific standing as a kind of most emblematic building associated with the college.”

Columbia has integrated its history of protest in its academic coursework, teaching it as a module in several history and American studies courses. The University now teaches a class called Columbia 1968, which is “centered on the student and community protests” of the University and Morningside Heights.

Elisha Baker, CC ’26, co-chair of Columbia Aryeh—an activist pro-Israel student group—said that Columbia “glorified” the 1968 protests, which he thinks “cemented a kind of culture that puts academic inquiry and what I believe to be the real purpose of the University, which is teaching, research, and learning, obviously, into second or third priority.”

On April 29, 2024, Columbia College Dean Josef Sorett wrote in an email to admitted students that, “Consequently, demonstration, political activism and deep respect for freedom of expression have long been part of the fabric of our campus.”

“Throughout this past year, the unfolding conflict in Gaza has intensified the volume and tone of that activism,” Sorett wrote in his email, which came one day before the Hamilton occupation began.

Connelly noted that Columbia “features its history when recruiting students, and as a historian, I want to know, what’s wrong with that?” Connelly said. “If the history as they were presenting it was really tendentious, then I would have an issue with that. But the fact is, if they didn’t mention ’68, I would have an even bigger issue with that.”

He added that students want to be “be where they think history is happening or could happen.”

Students occupy Hamilton Hall in 1968. Photo by Columbia Daily Spectator

Chris Lanser, a college admissions counselor with IvyWise, stated that though he hasn’t worked with students who have explicitly chosen Columbia because of its student activism and engagement, some students “are going to choose Columbia because there’s stuff coming down, and they want to get in on that for a variety of reasons.”

Baker offered a different perspective.

“Columbia certainly attracts activists—student activists and scholar activists—and I believe that this is not good for the well-being of the University because when people focus more on promoting their particular social and political agendas, they put those agendas before their primary responsibilities as educators, as engagers, and as learners,” Baker said.

Columbia Global

Former University President Lee Bollinger, Law ’71, set out to make Columbia a global university. Bollinger established 11 global centers across four continents, including one in Tel Aviv, which has garnered criticism from the student body. Bollinger also spearheaded the World Leaders Forum initiative, inviting leaders of dozens of countries to address Columbia students on an annual basis.

Columbia is a global university through its outreach, but also through its diverse student body. As of fall 2024, the University enrolled 13,745 international students, over a third of the 35,769 students enrolled, which is the third-largest international student population of any U.S. university.

Some of Columbia’s most infamous protests have been about Columbia’s role in global conflicts and wars. In 1985, student activists led a three-week demonstration, including a blockade of Hamilton, to demand that the University divest from American companies doing business in South Africa, protesting apartheid. The University became the first Ivy League school to divest.

A police officer arresting a protester during the 1987 Hamilton Hall blockade. Photo by Columbia Daily Spectator

Columbia University Apartheid Divest, the student group that organized the encampments and the Hamilton occupation, wrote in an Instagram post that its movement was “in line with the historical student movements of 1968, 1985, and 1996, which Columbia repressed then and celebrates today.”

“At the time, those protesters faced the same repression and vilification that we do today—which only fueled more protests in turn,” Alwan wrote. “The cycle of encouraging activism and then cracking down on it has made Columbia the epicenter of student movements throughout history.”

In 1996, students demanded the creation of an ethnic studies department, observing a hunger strike and occupying Low Library and Hamilton. In 1999, Columbia launched the Center for the Study of Ethnicity and Race.

Martin described Columbia as one of the main centers for area studies, including the study of the Middle East.

Columbia launched the Institute for Israel and Jewish Studies in 1950, a center for the study of Jewish life, history, and culture. IIJS also collaborates with academic institutions in Israel, including the IIJS Israel Fellowship.

In 2010, Columbia launched the Center for Palestine Studies, which promotes the academic study of Palestine and honors the legacy of Edward Said, a Palestinian Columbia professor for 40 years, known for his writing on orientalism.

“The dissonance between the University’s rich Palestinian scholarship and the administration’s anti-Palestinian repression has been profoundly jarring,” Alwan wrote. “This contradiction—between what is taught and what is policed—has fueled a vibrant culture of dissent and protest.”

After President Donald Trump sent a list of demands to the University as preconditions for negotiations about canceled federal funding, the University committed to placing the Middle Eastern, South Asian, and African studies department, IIJS, Center for Palestine Studies, and global centers in Tel Aviv and Amman under review.

The Trump administration has also targeted international affiliates at Columbia through detentions, attempted deportations, and visa revocations. The federal government targeted most of these individuals for their participation in pro-Palestinian protests.

Photo by Stella Ragas / Photo Editor

“Right-wing politicians recognize the nexus between critical theory and protest, which is precisely why they are cracking down on higher education and academic freedom,” Alwan wrote. “Ever since Congress began its McCarthyist investigations, the classroom has felt less like a space for free inquiry and more like a panopticon. Now, with the MESAAS department under receivership, even the university’s institutional independence is under siege.”

Immigration and Customs Enforcement detained Palestinian activists Mahmoud Khalil, SIPA ’24, on March 8 and Mohsen Mahdawi, GS ’25, on April 14, who are both lawful permanent residents. U.S. District Judge Geoffrey Crawford ordered Mahdawi’s release on bail on April 30.

The federal government has also attempted to deport several other affiliates, including Ranjani Srinivasan, a doctoral candidate at the Graduate School of Architecture, Planning and Preservation, Yunseo Chung, CC ’26, and Rumeysa Öztürk, TC ’20. The Department of Homeland Security alleged that these students participated in pro-Hamas activities, though they did not specify what those activities were.

The Trump administration has instructed Citizenship and Immigration Services to examine visa applicants’ social media for “antisemitic activity.” University Provost Angela Olinto announced that four Columbia students had their visas revoked, though the Trump administration is reinstating hundreds of visas across the country.

Acting University President Claire Shipman, CC ’86, SIPA ’94, affirmed in a Monday email that “for Columbia, international is simply essential.”

As the University’s reputation for student activism grows, the national—and international—spotlight has intensified.

“Why is everybody so interested in what’s happening on the Columbia campus?” Martin asked. “It must matter, not just to members of the Columbia community or to our immediate neighbors, but it must matter in the world, what happens on this campus.”
